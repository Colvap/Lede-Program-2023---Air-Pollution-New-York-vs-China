How Bad Could It Be?

Comparing Air Pollution in China and New York

Alex Colville - June 10th, 2023



On June 6th, a cluster of wildfires drifted down from Canada into New York City. The city's smog was "worst in the world" at the time - the air quality index reaching 200 during the night of June 6th.

But 'worst' is relative. China's smog is well-documented - a score of 200 AQI (Air Quality Index) is mild for some cities. Beijing's air quality index breaks above 50 - the average level considered safe by the WHO - frequently.

That's a serious problem. The WHO now recognizes air pollution as a major health threat worldwide, prolongued exposure causing around 7 million premature deaths each year.

China's cheap manufacturing industry, lax environmental regulation and dependence on coal-fired power stations during its economic boom meant smog was ubiquitous from the 1990s to the late 2010s.

But in recent years the government has taken steps to reduce air pollution, cutting down on coal-fired power stations and strengthening regulations on air pollution.

But it doesn't always work. The city of Huludao in China's industrial north-east still has a reputation for having 'the worst' air pollution in the country.

The city's chemical processing plants emit harmful gases as a waste product, locals complaining of regular sore throats, streaming eyes, and even nosebleeds.

It got so bad by 2022 that Chinese environmental artist "Nut Brother" launched a campaign to help the city.

In the past, 'the worst' levels of air pollution in New York have been nowhere near China's "worst". But climate change, and the wildfires that come with it, mean cities across the US (including San Francisco) can expect both a new normal, and a new 'worst'.

For this project, I extracted data from The World Air Quality Index Project for four days in New York over the past four years with the highest air pollution, and saw how these compared to the worst four days over the past four years in Beijing and Huludao.

This data was run through Pandas software, then turned into a chart using Datawrapper. Air pollution levels were measured by the AQI level of pm2.5 in the air, a particle created by burning coal, petrol, and wood - linked to increased rates of lung and cardiovascular disease when breathed in over a long period of time.





When put together, there is a gradual convergence over four years - Huludao's worst day so far in 2023 is around half its personal worst in 2020, while New York's worst day has risen to meet the worst levels of these two Chinese cities.

Measuring 'the worst' does not account for distribution or averages of air pollution....





We are also only halfway through 2023, making for an incomplete data-point for this year - the worst in Beijing or Huludao could be over the winter, when coal-fired power stations increase production to warm people's homes.

This is more about perceptions. Wildfires are expected to grow in frequency and severity as global temperatures rise. Meanwhile, China has dramatically cut back its air pollution. In the future, perhaps America's 'worst' will be more regularly on par with China's.
